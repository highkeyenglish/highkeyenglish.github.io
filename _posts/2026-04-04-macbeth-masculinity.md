## Masculinity in 'Macbeth'
This is one of my favourite themes in <i>Macbeth</i> because it encourages students to begin to construct more complex arguments around socialised gender. 'Masculinity' is a remarkably pliable and loaded term, and yet it often comes to signify nothing at all. You're wrestling with Jacobean understandings of masculinity, with the added layer of complexity being the twenty-first century hyperawareness of such terms being socially constructed - biological expectations of men are slowly being decoupled from their social expectations. (Simply because men are, on average, physically stronger than women, it does not then follow that they need be physically aggressive.) It is equally important that students avoid reductive arguments about Jacobean (and indeed eleventh-century Scottish) masculinity. As we'll see, it can take many nuanced forms, and isn't totally validating the hack and slash warrior lifestyle - there's room for sensitivity, compassion, morality, emotion and tears. 

In <i>Macbeth</i>, we encounter a diverse portfolio of characters, men and women, whom all represent varying degrees of masculinity. Of course, we know it as a largely harmful concept - toxic masculinity. Lady Macbeth weaponises her husband's insecurities around his masculinity in order to coax him into their murderous scheme, as we see in Act 1  Scene 7 (further explored in my slides). Lady Macbeth herself is an advocate of toxic masculinity even within herself, wishing to purify herself of supposed feminine weakness. Macbeth's poisonous, ambitious masculinity later on in the play is positioned as a foil to Macduff, who argues that he must 'feel' the murder of his entire family 'as a man'. Banquo embodies a patrilineal masculinity - the selfless father, willing to sacrifice himself for the future of his bloodline. He is also loyal comrade and friend, with a moral compass sharper than a needle's point. (This is interesting given being in touch with your morals is often pinned down as a 'feminine' trait.) Young Siward, meanwhile, is said to have 'died like a man' fighting Macbeth, having successfully proven himself in battle for an honest cause.

As you can see, there's loads to go off here.
