## Masculinity in 'Macbeth'
This is one of my favourite themes in <i>Macbeth</i> because it encourages students to begin to construct more complex arguments around socialised gender. 'Masculinity' is a remarkably pliable and loaded term, and yet it often ends up signifying nothing at all. You're wrestling with Jacobean conceptions of masculinity (and femininity by extension), with the added layer of complexity being the twenty-first century hyperawareness of such terms being socially constructed - biological expectations of men are slowly being decoupled from their social expectations. (Simply because men are, on average, physically stronger than women, it does not then follow that they need be physically aggressive.) 

It is equally important that students avoid reductive arguments about Jacobean (and indeed eleventh-century Scottish) masculinity. As we'll see, it isn't entirely validating the hack and slash warrior lifestyle - there's room for sensitivity, compassion, morality, emotion and tears. 

In <i>Macbeth</i>, we encounter a diverse portfolio of characters, men and women, whom all represent varying degrees of masculinity. We know it can be harmful, and we call that toxic masculinity. Lady Macbeth weaponises her husband's insecurities around his own masculinity in order to coax him into carrying out the murder, as we see in Act 1 Scene 7 (further explored in the slides). Lady Macbeth is both a victim and proponent of toxic masculinity, scorning her sex for its weakness. Macbeth's poisonous, ambitious masculinity later on in the play is positioned as a foil to Macduff, who argues that he must 'feel' the murder of his entire family 'as a man'. 

Banquo embodies a patrilineal masculinity - the selfless father, willing to sacrifice himself for the future of his bloodline. He is also a loyal comrade with a moral compass sharper than a needle's point. (This is interesting given that being in touch with your morals is often pinned down as a 'feminine' trait.) Young Siward, meanwhile, is said to have 'died like a man' fighting Macbeth, having successfully proven himself in battle for an honest cause.

## Teaching Materials
These materials can be tailored to students of any ability level. It has been designed for tuition (focusing on individual students) but can be adapted for a classroom setting if needed. 

During this lesson. students will:

<ul>
    <li>Learn preliminary vocabulary necessary to the topic</li>
    <li>Explore changing definitions of masculinity</li>
    <li>Watch a 'live lesson' of Act 1 Scene 7 and make notes about performance and line delivery</li>
    <li>Conduct close reading of an extract from Act 1 Scene 7</li>
    <li>Explore the theme of masculinity within the play</li>
    <li>Review an exemplar essay paragraph on the theme of masculinity</li>
</ul>
